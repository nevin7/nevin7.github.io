The Background

I've spent a decade making sure
knowledge doesn't get lost.

My career started in libraries and archives — places where organizing, describing, and making knowledge findable is the whole job. What I learned there was that the hard problem isn't storing information; it's giving it enough structure that other people (and now, other systems) can reliably understand and use it.

That thread runs through everything I've done since: building taxonomies at Cisco to govern metadata across enterprise content systems, designing ontologies at Boeing for aerospace knowledge management, and developing the semantic infrastructure at the International Monetary Fund that connected structured financial data across global teams.

The work has always been the same at its core — take complex domain knowledge that lives informally in people's heads or inconsistently across systems, and give it the formal structure it needs to be governed, reused, and trusted.

"The hard problem isn't storing information — it's giving it enough structure that other systems can reliably understand and use it."

What's changed recently is that the stakes of doing this well have gone up considerably. AI agents operating in finance, healthcare, and regulated industries are only as reliable as the domain knowledge underneath them. The field now has a name for what I've been doing — AI grounding — and a genuine shortage of people who know how to do it properly.

Education

Two graduate degrees.
One in information. One in climate.

My Master's in Library and Information Science from the University of Arizona gave me the foundational framework: information architecture, metadata standards, controlled vocabularies, and the theory behind how knowledge gets organized and retrieved. It's still the core of everything I do technically.

The second degree — a Master's in Climate Science and Solutions from Northern Arizona University — is a different story. It came from genuine curiosity about environmental data and modeling, and it sharpened something the first degree didn't: how to work with complex, uncertain, multidisciplinary knowledge and still produce something actionable. Climate science is a domain where the stakes of imprecise terminology and inconsistent data are unusually high. That experience informs how I think about semantic precision.

Together they make for an unusual combination — rigorous information science training alongside real scientific domain experience — that turns out to be well-suited to building knowledge systems for complex, regulated domains.

Going Independent

The job market is slow.
The need for this work isn't.

I'm currently available for both consulting engagements and full-time roles. The consulting path grew from a practical observation: the teams building AI products in regulated industries right now are moving fast, but very few of them have anyone on staff who understands formal knowledge representation. They're building on informal, inconsistent domain knowledge and wondering why their agents keep getting things wrong.

My approach is to embed with AI or data teams — usually for a defined engagement — and build the knowledge layer they're missing: a governed taxonomy, a formal ontology, a grounding data model. Something that persists and improves over time, rather than being rebuilt from scratch every time a new model is deployed.

If a full-time role is the right fit, I'm open to that too. What I'm looking for is work where the semantic layer is taken seriously — not treated as documentation overhead, but as core infrastructure for reliable AI.

How I Work

Principles that shape
every engagement.

After ten years of this work across very different organizations, a few things have become consistent about how I approach it.

Governance is not an afterthought

A taxonomy or ontology without stewardship, lifecycle states, and change management isn't an asset — it's technical debt. I build governance in from the start, not as a box to check at the end.

Standards exist for good reasons

SKOS, OWL, FIBO, HL7 — these aren't bureaucratic constraints. They're accumulated wisdom about how to make knowledge interoperable across systems and organizations. I use them because they work.

Precision serves everyone

Vague definitions feel inclusive but create downstream failures. I push for semantic precision — clear scope notes, explicit usage constraints, defined boundaries — because ambiguity in a knowledge model becomes hallucination in an AI system.

Business and technical must connect

The most common failure mode is a semantically correct artifact that nobody in the business understands or uses. I spend as much time on stakeholder alignment and plain-language documentation as on the formal modeling itself.
